# Power Laws (80/20)

> "A few things account for most of the results."

## Core Concept

**Power Laws** describe distributions where a small number of causes produce a disproportionately large share of effects. The famous "80/20 rule" (Pareto Principle) is one example: 80% of outcomes come from 20% of inputs.

Understanding power laws transforms how you allocate time, resources, and attention.

## The Pareto Principle

In 1896, economist Vilfredo Pareto noticed that 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the population. He found this pattern repeatedly:
- 80% of peas from 20% of pods
- 80% of wealth from 20% of people

This isn't magic—it's a natural property of many distributions.

### Business

- 80% of bugs from 20% of code
- 80% of sales from 20% of salespeople

### Personal Life

### Creative Work

- One unicorn pays for many failures

## The Mathematics

Power law distribution:
```
P(x) = Cx^(-α)
```

Where α determines how extreme the concentration is.

**In plain terms:** The top few are orders of magnitude larger than the rest.

### Normal (Bell Curve) vs Power Law

| Bell Curve | Power Law |
|------------|-----------|
| Most things cluster around average | Few outliers dominate |
| Extremes are rare and bounded | Extremes are common and unbounded |
| Height of humans | Wealth of humans |
| Test scores | Book sales |
| Temperature variation | Earthquake magnitude |

## Why Power Laws Exist

### Preferential Attachment

"Rich get richer" dynamics:
- Popular things become more popular
- Successful people get more opportunities
- Connected nodes get more connections

### Multiplicative Effects

Small differences compound:
- 10% better → 10% more customers → 10% more referrals → ...
- Advantages multiply over time

### Winner-Take-All Markets

Some markets have extreme concentration:
- One search engine dominates
- One social network wins
- One best-seller sells millions

## Implications for Decision Making

### 1. Find the Vital Few

Not all inputs are equal:
- Identify the 20% that produces 80%
- Double down on what works
- Cut or minimize the rest

### 2. Don't Spread Evenly

Equal allocation across unequal opportunities is wasteful:
- Invest more in top performers
- Spend more time on high-value activities
- Focus resources where returns are highest

### 3. Expect Extreme Outcomes

In power law domains:
- A few efforts will drive most results
- Many efforts will produce little
- This is normal, not failure

### 4. Seek Asymmetric Opportunities

Look for situations where:
- Upside is unbounded
- Downside is limited
- A few wins can pay for many failures

## Applying the 80/20 Rule

### Step 1: Identify the Category

What are you optimizing?
- Customer relationships
- Product features
- Time allocation
- Revenue sources

### Step 2: Measure Distribution

What produces what?
- Which customers generate most revenue?
- Which activities produce most results?
- Which efforts are wasted?

### Step 3: Concentrate on the Vital Few

Reallocate toward high-performers:
- More time with best customers
- More resources on winning products
- More attention on effective activities

### Step 4: Minimize or Eliminate the Trivial Many

Reduce or cut low-performers:
- Fire bad customers
- Sunset poor products
- Stop ineffective activities

## 80/20 Within 80/20

The principle often applies recursively:
- 20% of customers → 80% of revenue
- 20% of that 20% (4%) → 80% of that 80% (64%)
- 4% of customers → 64% of revenue

This means the very top is even more important than 80/20 suggests.

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Treating All Inputs Equally

Splitting time/resources evenly across unequal opportunities.

**Fix:** Measure, identify winners, concentrate resources.

### 2. Ignoring the Power Law

Expecting normal distribution when power law applies.

**Fix:** Recognize which domains follow power laws.

### 3. Optimizing the Wrong Things

Working on the 80% that produces 20%.

**Fix:** Constantly re-evaluate what's driving results.

### 4. Cutting Too Aggressively

The 80% isn't worthless—it may have strategic value.

**Fix:** Understand before eliminating.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Distribution is unequal** - A few things produce most results
2. **Find the vital few** - Identify what drives 80% of outcomes
3. **Concentrate resources** - Invest disproportionately in winners
4. **Minimize the trivial many** - Don't waste resources on low-impact
5. **It's recursive** - The very top matters even more
